Introduction

This project analyses data from the Coffee Quality Database (CQD), which contains information on coffee batches and their quality-related characteristics. The aim is to investigate which features of coffee, such as aroma, flavor, acidity, defects and altitude, influence whether a batch is classified as Good or Poor.

To answer this question, the dataset is first cleaned and explored using descriptive summaries and visualisations. A generalised linear model (GLM) is then used to examine how these variables are associated with coffee quality classification.

coffee <- coffee %>%
  distinct() %>%
  mutate(
    Qualityclass = as.factor(Qualityclass),
    country_of_origin = as.factor(country_of_origin)
  )

The dataset was inspected using glimpse() and summary() to understand the structure and distributions of the variables. Missing values were checked using colSums(is.na()), and duplicate rows were identified using duplicated(). Duplicate rows were removed to improve data quality before analysis.

coffee_clean <- coffee %>%
  filter(!is.na(country_of_origin),
         !is.na(aroma),
         !is.na(flavor),
         !is.na(acidity),
         !is.na(category_two_defects),
         !is.na(altitude_mean_meters),
         !is.na(harvested),
         !is.na(Qualityclass)) %>%
  filter(altitude_mean_meters > 0,
         altitude_mean_meters < 3000)

The dataset also contained missing values and unrealistic values in the altitude variable. To improve data quality, rows with missing values in key variables were removed. In addition, only observations with altitude between 0 and 3000 meters were retained, as extremely large altitude values are not plausible for coffee-growing regions. After cleaning, the number of observations was reduced from 1144 to 919.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Distribution of quality class

coffee_clean %>%
  count(Qualityclass) %>%
  ggplot(aes(x = Qualityclass, y = n, fill = Qualityclass)) +
  geom_col() +
  labs(
    title = "Distribution of Coffee Quality Class",
    x = "Quality Class",
    y = "Count"
  )

This bar chart shows the number of coffee batches classified as Good and Poor. The two classes are relatively balanced, although the number of Good batches is slightly higher than the number of Poor batches. This suggests that class imbalance is unlikely to be a major issue in the later logistic GLM, since neither category overwhelmingly dominates the dataset.

Aroma by quality class

ggplot(coffee_clean, aes(x = aroma, fill = Qualityclass)) +
  geom_histogram(alpha = 0.6, bins = 20, position = "identity") +
  labs(
    title = "Distribution of Aroma Scores by Quality Class",
    x = "Aroma Score",
    y = "Frequency"
  )

This histogram compares aroma scores across the two quality classes. The distribution for Good coffee is shifted slightly to the right, indicating that Good batches tend to have somewhat higher aroma scores than Poor batches. Although the two distributions overlap considerably, aroma still appears to contain useful information for distinguishing between the two classes and is therefore a plausible predictor in the later GLM.

Flavor by quality class

ggplot(coffee_clean, aes(x = flavor, fill = Qualityclass)) +
  geom_histogram(alpha = 0.6, bins = 20, position = "identity") +
  labs(
    title = "Distribution of Flavor Scores by Quality Class",
    x = "Flavor Score",

This plot compares flavor scores between Good and Poor coffee batches. Similar to aroma, the Good group appears to have a slightly higher concentration of observations at the upper end of the flavor scale. While there is substantial overlap between the two groups, the pattern suggests that higher flavor scores are associated with a greater probability of a batch being classified as Good, so flavor is likely to be an important explanatory variable.

Acidity by quality class

ggplot(coffee_clean, aes(x = acidity, fill = Qualityclass)) +

This histogram shows the distribution of acidity scores by quality class. The Good group appears to be centred at slightly higher acidity values, although the difference is less pronounced than for aroma or flavor. This suggests that acidity may still contribute to explaining coffee quality classification, but its effect may be weaker than some of the other sensory variables.

This boxplot compares the distribution of category two defects between coffee batches classified as Good and Poor. The Poor group appears to have a slightly higher median number of defects and greater variability than the Good group. There are also more extreme values in the Poor group. This suggests that category two defects may be associated with coffee quality classification and should be considered as a candidate predictor in the later GLM.

Altitude and aroma

ggplot(coffee_clean, aes(x = altitude_mean_meters, y = aroma, color = Qualityclass)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.7) +
  labs(
    title = "Altitude and Aroma Score by Quality Class",
    x = "Altitude (meters)",
    y = "Aroma Score"
  )

This scatterplot shows the relationship between altitude and aroma score, with points coloured by quality class. Most observations are concentrated within a similar altitude range, and the association between altitude and aroma does not appear strongly linear. However, Good batches tend to cluster at slightly higher aroma values across much of the altitude range. This suggests that altitude may provide some additional information, although its role is likely to be weaker and less direct than that of the sensory variables.

The correlation matrix summarises the relationships among the main numeric variables. Aroma, flavor and acidity are moderately to strongly positively correlated, which is expected because these sensory measures reflect related aspects of coffee quality. By contrast, altitude and category two defects show much weaker relationships with the sensory variables, suggesting that they may capture different dimensions of coffee production and quality. This is useful for the later GLM, as it indicates that some predictors may contain overlapping information while others may contribute more distinct explanatory value.

Overall, the exploratory analysis suggests that sensory variables, particularly aroma, flavor and acidity, differ between coffee batches classified as Good and Poor. Among these, aroma and flavor appear to show the clearest separation, while acidity shows a weaker but still noticeable difference. In addition, higher defect counts are more common among Poor batches, and altitude may provide some additional contextual information even though its relationship with quality appears less direct. These findings suggest that both sensory and production-related variables should be considered in the later GLM modelling the probability that a coffee batch is classified as Good rather than Poor.
